The Other Kind of Machine Vision

A µW, private, reversible perception-and-memory stack for the edge the cloud can't reach — and the person the cloud shouldn't see.

Millisecond Era · public thesis · 2026

The thesis

Mainstream AI perceives the world frame by frame and remembers it in the cloud. It is expensive, always watching, and the record it keeps is permanent, copyable, and not yours. Animals don't work this way. A frog — and your own retina — perceives by change: the static world is discarded almost for free, and attention flares only when something moves. And human memory is private, editable, and forgettable — you hold it, you revise it, you let it go.

We built the second kind. Four parts, one chain:

  1. a µW always-on change-attention layer that coasts through static scenes for almost nothing and wakes only on change;
  2. a predictive-surprise router that decides when to look thoroughly and which eye to use — spending recognition cost only on what reality fails to predict;
  3. a private, reversible, deterministic, forgettable on-device memory — bind a moment, recall it exactly, subtract it to forget it, merge and cleanly separate across devices, all bit-exact;
  4. a microwatt ReRAM-CIM chip that runs all of the above for weeks on a coin cell, on-device, with no cloud round-trip.

The moat is physics, not intelligence. We do not claim to be smarter than the cloud, and we do not try to out-recognize it. Our edge is four properties the cloud structurally cannot offer: µW · private · reversible · deterministic. That combination serves two markets the cloud was never built for — the edge it can't reach (off-grid, solar, weeks-on-a-battery) and the person it shouldn't see (the surveilled body, the right to be forgotten as a real operation).

1. The shift: change is the signal, and frugal-then-thorough beats both

Two ideas, both concrete.

Change is the signal. A conventional camera-plus-model digests every frame whether or not anything happened — burning power and bandwidth to re-confirm that the kitchen is still empty. The frog inverts this: the static scene is free, and the system only pays attention when the world changes. In our stack this is a near-free gate (CHANGE_THR = 0.92): if a frame is too similar to the last stored one, the system coasts — stores nothing, wakes nothing — at a projected sub-2µW. The road at 3am, the transformer yard, the sleeping infant: these are predictable scenes, and predictable scenes are nearly free.

Frugal first, thorough on surprise — the best of both. Human perception is cheap because it's lazy, but it is not blind: when something genuinely unexpected happens, attention floods in and looks hard. We reproduce that "flash on surprise." The cheap gate runs forever; when it fires, a predictive-residual test asks not "did a lot move?" but "did reality diverge from what I believed would happen?" Raw motion saturates under chaos — when everything is bursty, nothing stands out and the system under-looks (measured F1 collapse to 0.678 at high volatility). Surprise-as-prediction-error cannot saturate: it fires exactly on appear / disappear / swap / scene-switch, and it restored the lost accuracy in test. Only then — and only on the surprising region — does a heavier eye run.

The result is the combination nature and the datacenter each only half-have: the frugality of an animal and the exhaustiveness of a machine. The cheap gate gives us animal-grade power budgets; the surprise flash gives us machine-grade thoroughness exactly when it matters — and, honestly, only then. (No free lunch in chaos: when the world is genuinely unpredictable the router looks often and the µW advantage shrinks. We don't hide this; the law is "the frog wins when the world is predictable," which is most always-on scenes, not all.)

2. The moat, one line each

We claim exactly four things, and nothing more:

  • µW — sparse change-attention projects to ~1.6µW, roughly 364× cheaper than dense frame-by-frame processing. This is what turns a 7-day wearable into a 7-week one, and a tethered sensor into a solar-autonomous node.
  • Private — everything runs on-device; the raw frame/audio is discarded after summarizing into an embedding. No raw video stream leaves the device. There is no honeypot to leak, subpoena, or weaponize, because there is no center.
  • Reversible / forgettable — the memory is one summed vector; forgetting is an exact subtraction (M = M − record), undo is an exact re-add, and a counterfactual is a preview that's never committed. Right-to-be-forgotten becomes an operation, not a deletion request a server may ignore. A neural network cannot do this — you cannot cleanly subtract one memory from a trained net without retraining.
  • Deterministic / auditable — every operation is bit-exact at D=30k, seeded and reproducible. An auditor can verify what was recorded, when, and that it has not been altered. LLMs hallucinate; this does not.

And — stated plainly, because it is the discipline that keeps us honest — we do not out-recognize anyone. Turning messy pixels and audio into clean symbolic labels is the cloud's lane, and the cloud is better at it; that is fine. We consume those clean keys behind a stable contract and own the back-end algebra and the physics floor — the parts the front-end model can't touch. Computation is table stakes. Physics is the moat.

5. Honest boundaries

We publish our own corrections, and we refuse "faster/smarter than the cloud" claims. The honest ledger:

  • Recognition is the bottleneck, and it's not ours. Turning messy pixels/audio into clean atomic keys is the L1 front-end — the cloud and tiny on-device models win there. Misrecognition upstream becomes a confident-but-wrong key downstream; our contract makes that seam explicit and auditable, but it does not fix recognition. We consume clean keys; we don't manufacture them.
  • Much of the sensing is commodity. Event cameras (DVS / Prophesee / Samsung) already do change-only sensing in silicon — we are not reinventing the event camera. Industrial vibration monitoring (Emerson, Siemens), fall detection, soil sensors, dissolved-gas analysis: all mature. Our gap is the combination they don't offer — µW + private + reversible + on-device + federated, with no central honeypot.
  • What's already owned vs. what we add. Event cameras and cloud VLMs own raw perception; our addition is the predictive-residual surprise (relative to a belief, not raw event volume), the task-demand + periodic triggers that close change-detection's blind spot (a stove left on never changes — pure change-detection would never look again), and the reversible/federated/deterministic back-end behind it.
  • Determinism is a different axis from accuracy. We do not trade for higher accuracy — we trade for auditability and reversibility. Where the cloud is more accurate, good: swap it in behind the contract.
  • The chip is the open unknown. The µW power story rests on a ReRAM-CIM floor that is moving lab → prototype; density is the honest unknown. A few entries (e.g., petabyte-scale particle-detector triggering) are aspirational analogies at scale, flagged as such, not hidden. No vaporware.

6. Why now

Four curves are converging, and they all point the same way.

  • Regulation is creating demand for auditable, reversible AI. GDPR's right-to-be-forgotten, the EU AI Act, SR 11-7 and FDA determinism requirements all want what cloud LLMs structurally can't give: a model whose memory can be provably deleted and audited. Reversibility-as-an-operation is becoming a compliance requirement, not a nicety.
  • Wearable power budgets are tightening. Always-on glasses, hearables, and medical wearables are pinned by battery and bandwidth. A 364×-cheaper change-attention front-end is the difference between a feature that ships and one that drains in a day.
  • ReRAM-CIM is crossing lab → prototype. The physics floor that makes µW-on-a-coin-cell real is arriving now — early enough that the architecture and the contract matter, late enough that it's no longer fiction.
  • The global majority wants tools it owns. Four billion people with unreliable grids, seasonal connectivity, and a justified distrust of data extracted to Silicon Valley do not want a cloud subscription — they want a device that works through a three-month blackout and keeps their record local. That is exactly the lane the cloud was never built to serve.

The telos

The point is not to beat the cloud, and it is not to build a better surveillance machine. The point is to take care of the human being — especially the human the cloud can't reach and shouldn't see. Privacy here is not a setting; it is liberation — a memory the state, the employer, or the abuser cannot read, and a right to be forgotten that is a subtraction, not a promise. We will build it with the red lines or not build it at all.

The edge the cloud can't reach. The person the cloud shouldn't see. The memory that is finally yours.
