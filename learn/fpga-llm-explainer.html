A tiny LLM on a ¥205 FPGA  — how a real language model runs on $28 of silicon, from first principles

A ¥205 chip is writing English — on the metal itself

There is a tiny language model living inside a ¥205 (~$28) circuit board — a salvaged Bitcoin-miner board with a Xilinx Zynq-7010 chip. No GPU, no cloud, no internet. It generates text on the silicon at a measured ~13,677 characters per second. This page takes you all the way down: from "what is the model" to "how do the electrons actually do it," one playable idea at a time.

The chip has two halves
PS — the "computer" side

A small ARM CPU running Linux (PYNQ). It boots from an SD card, like a Raspberry Pi. It's the manager: it loads our circuit, reads results out, talks to your laptop. It does not run the model.

PL — the "blank Lego" side

A field of reprogrammable logic (the FPGA fabric). It starts as un-wired Lego bricks. We load a "bitstream" that wires those bricks into our circuit — the language model. This is where thinking happens.

The ternary trick — multiplication without a multiplier

A normal neural net stores each weight as a precise decimal (e.g. 0.0314). Multiplying by it needs a real multiplier circuit — expensive in silicon. Our trick: force every weight to be just −1, 0, or +1 (three values → "ternary"). Now "multiply" becomes trivial:

×(+1) = keep the number ×(−1) = flip its sign ×(0) = skip it

That's it — add, subtract, or ignore. No multiplier needed. This is the single biggest reason the model is tiny and fast. The core math of a neural layer is the MAC (multiply-accumulate): "for each output, sum up the inputs, keeping or negating each." With ternary weights it's the add/subtract/skip you see below.

No decimals allowed — fixed-point integers

Hardware hates fractions. A circuit that handles "3.14159" (floating-point) is big and slow. So the whole model uses only whole numbers (integers) and remembers, by convention, "the real value is this integer divided by some power of two." That's fixed-point.

Analogy. Instead of writing "$3.14", everyone agrees to count in cents: store 314, and remember to divide by 100 at the end. Same information, but now it's just integers — which circuits love. Our "cents" is dividing by powers of two (a free bit-shift), e.g. ACT_SHIFT=8 means "divide by 256."

So a ternary matrix-multiply (real code, matvec_ref) is: acc = Σ(±x) (the add/subtract/skip from section 3) → then one scale-and-shrink: out = round(acc × alpha >> 12). The alpha puts the magnitude back (since the weights lost it when we crushed them to ±1), and >>12 is "divide by 4096" — a shift, not a division.

The hard, curvy bits: lookup tables + clever tricks

Some operations aren't add/subtract — they're curves: RMSNorm (normalize by the root-mean-square), softmax (turn scores into a probability-like spread), SiLU (a smooth gate). Building these as exact circuits is expensive. Instead the chip uses:

  • Lookup tables (LUT) — precompute the curve into a small table and just read the answer. Real: silu_lut (512 entries), exp_lut (256), recip_norm (1024).
  • A Newton-style square root (bit-by-bit "restoring sqrt") for the RMS denominator.
  • A reciprocal LUT so dividing becomes "look up 1/x, then multiply" — and multiply-by-table is cheaper than a real divider.

That last step — value × reciprocal >> shift inside RMSNorm — is the chip's slowest path. Hold that thought; it's the star of the timing section.

The KV cache — how it remembers the last 8 characters

Attention lets each new character "look back" at earlier ones. But recomputing the past every step is wasteful. So the chip keeps a small notebook — the KV cache — storing a Key and a Value for each recent character. Ours holds T_MAX = 8 characters: a sliding 8-slot window.

Analogy. A ring of 8 sticky-notes on a wheel. Each new character writes its note on slot position mod 8. When you reach slot 8 you wrap back to slot 0, overwriting the oldest. The model always "sees" the last 8 notes — no more.
The ring of 8 — step the position forward

Real indexing (from the RTL & golden ref): write slot = pos % T_MAX; the "attention window" is positions max(0, pos−7) … pos. In hardware the cache is two small dual-port memories addressed layer×T_MAX×D + slot×D + dim — total 256 words.

Inside the silicon: the step-by-step machine

Software runs line by line. Hardware runs as a state machine (FSM): a circuit that, every clock tick, is in exactly one named "stage," does that stage's job, and moves to the next. The key insight. There is one MAC engine, and the FSM time-shares it: it runs the same hardware for Q, then K, then V, then the output projection, then both feed-forward matrices, then the head — over and over, for every layer. This is exactly why a bigger model is slower: more numbers must wait their turn through the same single engine.

The 17.9 ns lesson — and how pipelining beats it

The chip runs on a clock that ticks at a fixed rate. At 50 MHz, one tick = 20 nanoseconds (20 billionths of a second). The iron rule: every signal must travel through its logic and settle before the next tick. The slowest path in the entire design sets the speed limit for everything.

Our slowest path is that RMSNorm step from section 4: value × reciprocal (a multiply) feeding a long chain of shift-and-saturate logic. It takes ~17.9 ns. It just barely fits inside a 20 ns tick (~2 ns to spare) — so 50 MHz works. But 100 MHz means 10 ns ticks, and 17.9 > 10 → it would fail.

The tick budget — toggle the clock & the pipeline
Assembly-line analogy. One worker doing a 17-minute job can't go faster than every 17 minutes. Split it into two ~9-minute stations with a hand-off shelf (a "register") between them: now the line advances every 9 minutes — nearly twice as fast. Each item takes one extra step, but a new item finishes every short tick. That split is exactly what we did to the RMSNorm multiply (the rdiv-pipe versions in the file history) to climb toward 100 MHz.

This connects the three knobs: SIZE adds work, FIT limits how much logic you can spend, and SPEED is bought back by pipelining the slowest path. Section 11 lets you slide all three.

Where the −1 / 0 / +1 come from: training

The chip can't learn; it just runs fixed weights. Those weights are learned ahead of time on a normal computer, then crushed into ternary. Two stages:

Stage A — learn normally (full precision)

A normal small Transformer is trained on TinyStories text, filtered down to a 32-character vocabulary (space, newline, a–z, and a few punctuation marks). Real recipe (train_v3s_poc.py): optimizer AdamW, learning rate 3e-4 (cosine decay), ~4,000 steps, batch 64, sequence length 64, plain cross-entropy loss. At this point weights are ordinary decimals.

Stage B — ternarize (snap to −1 / 0 / +1)

After training, each weight matrix is converted: compute alpha = average(|weights|), then snap each weight — anything bigger than 0.7 × alpha becomes +1 (carrying the magnitude alpha in the per-matrix scale you met in section 4), anything below −0.7 × alpha becomes −1, and everything in the middle becomes 0. A higher threshold = more zeros = sparser, smaller, faster — but too high throws away real signal and the model gets worse. 0.7 is the tuned sweet spot. The final ~6,224 ternary weights are then packed 8-to-a-word and written into the .mem files the hardware reads.
