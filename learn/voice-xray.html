🩻 Voice X-Ray

What a "speech-to-text" model already knows about how you speak — before it knows what you said.
Honest box. Two kinds of thing live on this page. (1) The live gauges below run entirely in your browser with the Web Audio API — your microphone audio never leaves this page, nothing is uploaded or stored. (2) The measured science (the brain-floor map, the scorecard) comes from offline experiments on public datasets (RAVDESS, ESD), tested on speakers the model had never heard. Emotion read-out itself needs a model too big to run in a web page, so its numbers are reported, not run live here. Demo = proof-of-concept; the chip is a roadmap.

2 · The brain floors — what lives in each layer

We cut open three speech models (wav2vec2, WavLM, Whisper) layer by layer and probed what each layer can predict. One forward pass, tap a pipe at the right floor, get seven percepts — no extra model. Numbers below: WavLM, measured on RAVDESS, speaker-independent. UAR = balanced accuracy (chance shown); R² = regression fit.

Notice the gradient: the shallow floors hold raw sound (pitch, energy); the middle floors hold who is speaking; only the deep floors hold emotion and the actual words. The model learns to throw away "how" on its way to "what" — but the "how" is still in there, on the middle floors, for free.

3 · Three things nobody else does

"Emotion lives in speech-model layers" is known. Our angle is what you can do with it once you treat it as algebra you can run on a chip.

① Freeze-dry it onto a chip, almost losslessly.
The emotion features compress to ternary (-1/0/+1) chip format keeping ≥95% of the signal — Whisper's layers even round-trip at ≥100%. The physical basis for an emotion-aware µW chip that needs no cloud and almost no power. [MEASURED: 2 corpora × 3 models × 6 encodings × 3 seeds]

② One hour of voice → a 10 KB memory.
Each 10-second window's emotion fingerprint is bound to a time-key and summed into one vector. An hour = one 10 KB vector (raw audio is ~600 MB), and you can ask it "what was I feeling at minute 23?" — 85% correct. It stores the fingerprint, not a label, so a question you invent next month can be asked of memories recorded today — while the original audio is long gone. Privacy isn't a promise not to listen; it's physically unable to. [MEASURED: fidelity 0.85; scales to 1M-dim = a full day in 8 MB]

③ It sees through performance.
In one test the system missed acted anger almost entirely, but nailed the one moment of genuine delight with the highest confidence of the whole clip. If that holds (it's one sample — we don't over-claim), it's exactly what a coaching tool wants: faked confidence doesn't fool it; real ease earns the score. It also draws an honest line — restrained, low-arousal negatives (held-back anger, quiet sadness) are the hardest to read from sound alone, and those are exactly what a coach most needs. Knowing where the map ends is itself knowledge.

Why emotion, and why now

Bruce Lee had a line that stuck with me: the conscious mind doesn't drive behavior directly — emotion is the switch in between. Relaxed and positive, your ability amplifies; tense and afraid, it narrows. Emotion has bandwidth, and — this is the part people miss — it can be trained. It's the most under-rated, hardest-to-quantify thing in human performance. These hammers are the first time I could make it measurable, compressible, recordable, and re-queryable. The goal was never a smarter AI. It was a more private, portable, visible memory — to take care of the human being, especially where the cloud can't reach.
